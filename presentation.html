The problem with manual screening

Right now, recruiters are going through resumes one by one. It's slow, it's inconsistent, and the outcome depends heavily on who's reviewing and when. The same strong candidate gets a different result depending on the reviewer's mood, time pressure, and personal bias.

❌ Today
  • Recruiters spend 20–30 hrs screening per open role
  • No standard scoring — completely subjective
  • Good candidates missed due to keyword mismatch
  • Process doesn't scale when volume spikes
  • No audit trail — can't explain why someone was rejected
✅ With this system
  • Every resume gets a score in seconds
  • Consistent, repeatable ranking for every JD
  • Understands meaning — not just keyword matching
  • Scales to hundreds of resumes without extra effort
  • Transparent score breakdown per candidate

What the system does

You give it a job description and a folder of resumes. It reads every resume, understands what each candidate is about, compares that against what the role needs, and gives you a ranked shortlist with scores.


1
Parse & cleanSplits each resume into sections — Skills, Experience, Summary, Education. Normalises the text.
2
Understand, not matchConverts the JD and each resume section into meaning vectors using an AI model. "Built LLM agents" and "developed agentic AI systems" are treated as the same thing.
3
Score and rankEach section is compared against the JD and combined into a final relevance score. Skills and experience are weighted higher than education.

The technical choice — and why

The core question was: keyword matching or semantic understanding? I went with semantic. Here's why that matters for this problem.

Factor Sentence-Transformers (chosen) TF-IDF (baseline considered)
Understands meaning Yes — "LLM agent" ≈ "agentic AI" No — word frequency only
Handles synonyms & paraphrasing Yes No
Needs internet or paid API No — runs fully offline No
Speed ~50ms per resume Faster but less accurate
Works on varied language Yes — handles different writing styles Struggles with varied phrasing

Model: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 — lightweight, fast, strong on semantic similarity. No API cost, runs on any machine.

Not all sections are equal

A skills match should count more than an education match for a senior engineering role. The system scores each resume section separately and combines them using these weights.

Technical Skills
40%
Work Experience
40%
Profile / Summary
15%
Education
5%

These weights can be adjusted per role type — a fresh graduate role would weight education differently than a senior hire.

Does the ranking hold up?

The question isn't just whether individual labels are correct — it's whether the system ranks candidates in the right order so a recruiter reviewing the top results is spending time on the right people.

At scale with real labeled data, we'd track Ranking Quality (nDCG@K) and Recall@K as the primary metrics — making sure we're not just ranking correctly, but also not missing good candidates buried further down.

Path to production

This is a working POC. Here's what it takes to turn it into something the TA team uses day-to-day.

1
PDF & DOCX support — handle real resume formats, extract structured fields like current title, years of experience, and location
2
Hard filter layer — enforce minimum experience, required certifications, and location on top of the semantic score
3
Fine-tune on real data — use historical hire/no-hire decisions to adapt the model specifically to your recruiting language and standards
4
API + recruiter UI — wrap in FastAPI, deploy on AWS, build a simple interface so TA team can upload a JD and get a ranked shortlist without touching code
5
Feedback loop — collect recruiter accept/reject signals and retrain periodically. The system gets better the more it's used.
